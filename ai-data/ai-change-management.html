The Challenge

Why Government AI Initiatives Stall

Deloitte's 2026 State of AI in the Enterprise report found that insufficient worker skills remain the single biggest barrier to integrating AI into existing workflows. Government agencies face amplified versions of every adoption challenge: risk-averse cultures, complex stakeholder landscapes, union considerations, and public scrutiny of AI-driven decisions.

The result is predictable: agencies invest millions in AI platforms and models, only to see adoption plateau at 15-20% of the intended user base. Pilots succeed in controlled conditions but fail to scale because the organizational fabric -- culture, processes, incentives, and skills -- was never redesigned to absorb the change.

Top Adoption Barriers in Government:

  • 70% Resistance to change -- staff fear displacement or distrust AI outputs
  • 60% Skill gaps -- teams lack knowledge to use AI tools effectively
  • 50% Process misalignment -- workflows not redesigned for AI integration
  • 45% Leadership gaps -- sponsors cannot articulate the AI vision
Our Framework

ADKAR for Government AI Adoption

We apply the proven ADKAR change management framework, customized for the unique dynamics of government AI initiatives -- from union engagement to public accountability requirements.

Phase 1

Awareness

Build understanding of why AI is being introduced and what problems it solves. Address fears and misconceptions head-on through transparent, empathetic communication. In government, this means engaging union representatives, frontline supervisors, and citizen advocacy groups early -- before rumors fill the vacuum.

  • Stakeholder mapping and sentiment baseline assessment
  • Myth-busting communications (AI will not replace your job)
  • Leadership alignment workshops and talking point development
  • Town halls, Q&A sessions, and anonymous feedback channels
Phase 2

Desire

Create genuine motivation to participate in the change. Show staff how AI will make their work more effective and meaningful -- not eliminate their roles. Identify and empower champions and early adopters who can demonstrate the benefits firsthand to their peers.

  • Early adopter identification and champion network development
  • Peer-led demo sessions showing real time savings
  • Personal impact assessments (what changes for me?)
  • Incentive design: recognition, career development, and skill badges
Phase 3

Knowledge

Provide targeted training and resources to work effectively with AI. Role-specific learning paths ensure everyone -- from caseworkers using AI-assisted recommendations to analysts building dashboards -- has the skills they need to succeed.

  • Role-based curriculum development and delivery
  • Hands-on workshops with real AI tools and scenarios
  • Job aids, quick-reference guides, and video tutorials
  • AI literacy assessments with follow-up coaching
Phase 4

Ability

Support the critical transition from training to daily practice. Coaching, embedded help desks, and peer mentoring help staff apply new skills in real work situations. This phase addresses the "knowing-doing gap" that derails most technology rollouts.

  • On-the-job coaching and floor-walking support
  • AI help desk and escalation protocols
  • Peer mentoring and buddy system programs
  • Workflow redesign to embed AI into daily processes
Phase 5

Reinforcement

Sustain the change over time with systems that prevent regression. Recognition programs, success metrics, continuous improvement loops, and leadership visibility cement new ways of working and create a culture where AI fluency is the norm, not the exception.

  • Adoption dashboards and utilization tracking
  • Recognition programs and success story amplification
  • Continuous improvement sprints based on user feedback
  • Quarterly adoption reviews with leadership

The Business Case

Change Management Is Not Optional for Government AI

Government agencies operate under constraints the private sector does not face: civil service protections, collective bargaining agreements, public records requirements, and heightened scrutiny of automated decision-making. These factors make change management not just helpful but essential.

  • Protects public trust -- transparent change processes demonstrate responsible AI governance to citizens and oversight bodies
  • Navigates labor relations -- structured engagement with unions and employee groups builds support rather than opposition
  • Accelerates ROI -- every month of delayed adoption is a month of unrealized value from your AI investment
  • Scales beyond pilots -- a change-managed rollout creates reusable playbooks for every subsequent AI initiative

"We had already deployed the AI platform, but utilization was stuck at 18%. Brite.AI came in, ran stakeholder interviews, and uncovered that our caseworkers believed the tool was being used to monitor their performance.
